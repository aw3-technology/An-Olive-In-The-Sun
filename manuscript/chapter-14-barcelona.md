# Chapter 14
## Barcelona

A server passes with bruschetta on a wooden board, and the smell reaches me before the food does. Olive oil. Warm and grassy and so unmistakably Mediterranean that for a moment I am not standing in Northern California at all. I am somewhere else. Somewhere with narrower streets and hotter light and a version of myself I have spent a decade trying to forget.

The crowd has drifted. I notice this the way you notice weather changing, not with your mind but with your skin. One moment I am surrounded by people, their voices and perfume and the clink of glasses forming a kind of architecture around me, and the next moment the architecture is gone. They have moved toward the bar, toward the ceremony site, toward each other. I am standing alone near the edge of the terrace, and the aloneness registers in my body before my mind catches up. A tightening across my chest. A contraction in my stomach. The cellular memory of what it feels like when no one is coming.

I touch the hem of my dress. Smooth it. Touch it again. My fingers need something to hold.

The aloneness lasts only seconds. Someone will call my name. Someone always calls my name today. But my nervous system doesn't know that. My nervous system is in a hotel room in the Barri Gotic, twenty-two years old, watching the door and understanding, slowly, that it is not going to open.

---

Nadia rolled her own cigarettes the way some people sign their names: quickly, precisely, without looking. She kept the tobacco in a leather pouch she'd bought in Istanbul, and the papers were the thinnest I'd ever seen, translucent as onion skin, and she would lick the edge and seal it and bring the cigarette to her lips in a single motion that looked, every time, like something choreographed.

She was twenty-six when I met her. A freelance photographer. French mother, Algerian father, raised between Paris and Montreal, which gave her an accent that shifted depending on who she was talking to. She spoke French, English, and enough Arabic to order food and argue with cab drivers, and she moved through the world the way trilingual people sometimes do, as though language itself were a costume she could change. She had cropped dark hair she cut herself and a tattoo of a compass rose on her left wrist and she wore the same black jeans every day with different tops, simple and precise, the wardrobe of someone who had decided what she looked like and saw no reason to revisit the question.

I met her at a gallery opening in San Francisco. I was twenty-two, eight months out from the wreckage of Will, and I was standing in front of a photograph of an empty swimming pool, pretending to have an opinion about it, when she appeared beside me and said, in a voice that was both casual and certain, "It's better if you don't try to understand it."

I looked at her. She was looking at the photograph.

"The pool," she said. "It's not about the pool. It's about the light on the tiles."

She was right. I looked again and the photograph changed. I could see it now, the way the afternoon sun caught the dry mosaic at the bottom and turned it into something luminous. She had a way of doing that. Of shifting what you were looking at without moving anything except your attention.

We talked for two hours. She bought me a glass of wine I didn't finish. She asked me what I studied and when I said philosophy she laughed, not unkindly, and said, "So you think for a living. I look for a living. We should get along."

We did. For a while, we did.

---

Barcelona was her idea. She had a friend with an apartment in the Barri Gotic, two rooms above a guitar shop, and she wanted to spend a month shooting the city. She asked me to come the way she asked everything: as though the answer were already settled and she was just waiting for me to catch up.

I said yes the way I said everything to Nadia. Immediately. Without asking the questions I should have asked. What are we, exactly? What happens when the month is over? What do you do when someone needs you?

It did not occur to me to ask because I did not yet know that I would need her. And because needing someone felt, at twenty-two, like the thing I was trying most urgently to outgrow.

We flew into Barcelona on a Wednesday in late September. The plane descended over the coast and I pressed my forehead against the window and watched the Mediterranean appear below us, flat and impossibly blue, the kind of blue that seems artificial until you see it in person and realize that the artificial versions were always trying to imitate this. The city spread from the water's edge up into the hills, dense and ochre and glittering in the late afternoon, and Nadia leaned across me to take a photo through the scratched plastic of the airplane window, her shoulder against mine, her camera clicking twice, and I thought: this is what it feels like to be chosen.

Not loved, necessarily. Chosen. Selected from the available options by someone whose taste you trusted. Nadia had been to fourteen countries. She could have asked anyone. She asked me.

The apartment was small and bright and smelled like cedar and old paper. The floors were terra-cotta tile, uneven in places, cool under bare feet. One window faced the narrow street below, where the guitar shop's owner sat on a stool each morning playing flamenco scales that drifted up through the glass like something the building was dreaming. The other window faced a courtyard where someone had strung white lights between the balconies, and at night they swayed in the breeze off the water and made shadows that moved across our ceiling like small, slow fish.

Nadia unpacked her camera bags first. Three bodies, five lenses, a tripod that folded into something the size of a baguette. She laid them on the kitchen table the way a surgeon lays out instruments, each one in its place, each one chosen for a specific purpose. Then she opened the window and lit a cigarette and looked out at the street and said, "This will do."

And for a while, it did. More than that. For a while, it was beautiful.

---

I need to tell you about the city, because the city is part of the story. Not background. Character.

We walked everywhere. Barcelona is a city that asks you to look up, and I had spent most of my life looking down, scanning the ground in front of me, watching for what might go wrong. But Gaudi's buildings made looking down impossible. The Sagrada Familia rose above the neighborhood like something from a fever dream, its spires reaching and reaching, covered in carved fruit and stone animals and stained glass that threw color across the floor in pools of red and gold and violet that moved with the sun. I stood inside it with my neck craned back and my mouth slightly open and I felt something I did not have a word for. Not awe, exactly. Recognition. As though someone had built the inside of my mind and hung it with light.

Nadia photographed everything. The peeling shutters on the side streets. A woman hanging laundry on a balcony, her arms reaching up, a white sheet billowing behind her like a wing. The fish market in the Boqueria, where the vendors arranged their catch in spirals of silver and pink on beds of crushed ice, and the smell was the ocean concentrated into a single room. An old man on the Ramblas playing accordion with his eyes closed, his fingers moving across the buttons with the precision of someone who has played the same songs for forty years and still finds something new in them.

She shot three rolls of film a day, minimum. She was not sentimental about it. She did not talk about capturing moments or preserving beauty. She talked about light. Always light. Where it fell, how it moved, what it did to surfaces. She would stop in the middle of a sentence, mid-step, and raise her camera and take a single photograph of a shadow on a wall, and then keep walking as though nothing had interrupted her.

I loved watching her work. The concentration of it. The way the camera transformed her from a restless, mercurial person into someone entirely focused, entirely present. She was never more herself than when she was looking through a viewfinder. I understood this because I had felt it too, in different rooms, with different instruments. The philosophy lecture that silenced everything else. The page of Hegel that made the noise stop. We were both people who needed a frame to make the world bearable. Hers was a lens. Mine was a syllogism.

We ate dinner late, the way everyone in Barcelona ate dinner late, at ten or eleven, sitting at small tables on the sidewalk with glasses of cava and plates of patatas bravas and pan con tomate, bread rubbed with tomato and drizzled with olive oil, the oil pooling in the rough surface of the toast like something the bread had been waiting for. The air smelled like garlic and exhaust and jasmine from the planters on the balconies above. Nadia would order for both of us in rapid, confident Spanish that was grammatically imperfect and entirely effective, and I would sit across from her and think: I could stay here. Not in Barcelona, necessarily. But in this feeling. This specific frequency of being alive.

I was happy. Or something so close to it that the difference didn't matter.

---

What it meant to be with a woman. I should say something about that, because it was part of the story and not part of the story at the same time.

It was not a revelation. It was not the thing where the sky opens and the music swells and everything suddenly makes sense. It was more like finding a word for something I had always felt but never needed to name. I had been drawn to women since I was old enough to notice the quality of my attention, the way it sharpened around certain people, certain faces, certain kinds of beauty that had nothing to do with what I'd been taught to want. I noticed boys too. I noticed everything. The problem was never attraction. The problem was that no one had given me a language for it, and the languages I had been given were wrong.

Catholic school called it sin. The Jesuit college practiced careful silence, which was its own kind of condemnation, the way not mentioning something can be louder than naming it. The modeling world fetishized it for the camera, two girls kissing at a party while men watched, which was not queerness but performance, not identity but product. My sexuality existed in the gaps between every institution I had ever passed through, too real to ignore and too unscripted to announce.

When I told my mother I was seeing a woman, she said nothing. Not "I'm disappointed." Not "I'm happy for you." Nothing. She changed the subject to something about a coworker's retirement party, and the conversation moved on as though I had mentioned the weather. I could not tell whether her silence was acceptance or refusal, and I have never asked, because asking would mean admitting that I needed her response, and we had both spent years pretending we did not need things from each other.

My grandparents simply did not acknowledge it. Nadia did not exist in their vocabulary. When I visited their house, they asked about school, about work, about whether I was eating enough. They did not ask about my life. Their love had always been conditional on my legibility, and this was the first thing about me they could not read.

My sister shrugged. "Okay," she said, and went back to her phone. The most supportive response was also the most dismissive, and I could not decide whether it meant she truly didn't care or whether she had simply reached her capacity for absorbing new information about our family's deviations from the script.

Queerness was not a crisis. It was just one more way the life I was living failed to match the life I had been given. One more identity I had to assemble from scratch, without a model, without a template, without even the comfort of a dramatic origin story. I was bisexual and it was unremarkable and the unremarkableness of it was, in its own quiet way, the loneliest part.

---

The fever started on a Tuesday. I woke in the apartment above the guitar shop and my body was wrong. The kind of wrong that announces itself in your bones before it arrives in your throat, your stomach, your head. A heaviness. As though gravity had been recalibrated during the night and everything weighed more than it should.

By afternoon I could not leave the bed. The terra-cotta floor, which had been cool and pleasant, was now a frozen lake I could not cross. The sheets were soaked. My joints ached with a deep, cellular pain, the kind that makes you aware of the architecture of your own skeleton, every hinge and socket suddenly present and objecting. The light from the courtyard window, which had been beautiful, was now a blade.

Nadia brought water. She sat on the edge of the bed and pressed her hand to my forehead and said, "You're burning up." She went out and came back with ibuprofen and a can of Sprite from the corner store, and she sat beside me while I drank it, her hand on my knee, and for a few hours I thought: this is what it looks like when someone takes care of you. This is the part of the movie where the other person stays.

The next day she brought more water. She checked on me twice. She opened the window to let in air, then closed it when I said I was cold, then opened it again an hour later. She was trying. I could see the effort in her face, the way her jaw tightened slightly each time she entered the room, as though walking through the door required a small act of will.

By the third day the effort was wearing through.

She left early that morning with two camera bags and came back after dark. I had been alone for eleven hours. The guitar player downstairs had come and gone and come again. I had counted the tiles on the ceiling. I had slept and woken and slept and woken. I had tried to eat a banana and failed. I had not spoken to another person all day.

When she came back, she smelled like cigarettes and the city. Garlic and exhaust and the particular warmth of a day spent moving through sun-heated streets. She set her cameras down with the careful attention she gave them and then looked at me, in the bed, in the same position she had left me, and something crossed her face. Not anger. Not disgust. Something closer to suffocation. The look of a person standing at the edge of a room that is shrinking.

"How are you feeling?" she asked.

"The same."

She nodded. Poured herself a glass of wine. Stood at the window with her back to me.

"I found this street behind the cathedral," she said. "The light was incredible. Golden. Like the buildings were on fire."

I said nothing. She was describing a world I could not reach.

The fourth day she left earlier and came back later. The fifth day she ate dinner out. "With friends," she said, meaning people she had met. Nadia collected people the way she collected photographs, quickly and without attachment, drawn to the surface of things, the composition, the play of light and angle. She could make anyone feel like the most interesting person in the room. I knew this because she had done it to me.

On the sixth day she sat on the edge of the bed and said the thing I had been waiting for.

"I didn't come to Barcelona to sit in a hotel room."

She said it quietly. Without malice. The way you might state a fact about the weather or the time. She was not cruel. That is the thing I need you to understand. She was not cruel. She was limited. There is a difference, and the difference matters, because cruelty would have been easier to forgive. Cruelty comes from somewhere, has a shape, a reason, a wound you can identify and, with enough therapy, understand. Limitation is just a wall. Smooth, featureless, impossible to climb. She could not be needed. She could be wanted, admired, desired, enjoyed. But needed was a room she could not enter, and my illness had locked us both inside it.

"I'm going to Lisbon for a few days," she said. "Sofia has a place there. I'll be back before the end of the week."

I nodded. What else could I do? I could barely walk to the bathroom. I was not going to argue with someone about staying when staying was the one thing they could not do. I had watched my father leave. I had watched Mark destroy himself. I knew what it looked like when someone's capacity ran out. The shape of it was always the same. A door, a suitcase, a sentence that pretended to be temporary.

She packed a small bag. She kissed my forehead. She said she would call.

She did not call.

The text arrived a week later, after I had already understood. Two lines on a screen. She was sorry. She wasn't ready for this. She wished me well.

I read it lying on the narrow bed in the apartment above the guitar shop, where the flamenco scales still drifted up through the floor each morning like something that refused to acknowledge what had happened inside these walls. I read it twice, then set the phone on the nightstand, and the screen went dark, and the room was very quiet.

---

I stayed in Barcelona for ten more days. Not because I wanted to. Because my body would not let me leave.

The fever broke on the third day after Nadia left, but what replaced it was worse. A hollow, wrung-out weakness that made every task monumental. Walking to the pharmacy on the corner required the kind of planning other people reserved for international travel. Shoes. Keys. Wallet. The three flights of stairs down to the street, each step deliberate, my hand on the stone wall, the wall cool and rough under my palm. The pharmacist, a woman with reading glasses on a chain, who looked at my face and spoke slowly and kindly, the way you speak to someone very old or very young, and gave me electrolyte packets and something for the nausea and waved away my attempt at Spanish with a gentle hand.

I walked through the city. Not with purpose. With the aimless, shuffling gait of a person who cannot stay in a room any longer. The same streets Nadia had photographed were still beautiful. That was the cruelest part. The light still fell the way she had described it, golden and warm, turning the sandstone buildings into something that belonged in a painting. The Ramblas were still full of people walking slowly in the shade of the plane trees, eating ice cream, holding hands, performing the specific leisure of tourists in a city built for being looked at. The Mediterranean was still visible at the end of certain streets, a stripe of impossible blue between buildings, patient and indifferent, the way water is always indifferent.

I walked through all of it and felt invisible. Not unseen, exactly. Noticed, even. A woman walking alone draws looks in any city. But invisible in the way that matters. Unknown. Unconnected. A body moving through space without anyone on the other end of it, no one to call, no one expecting me back, no anchor.

The worst part was not the loneliness. The worst part was how familiar it felt. I had been alone before. I had been left before. The feeling was almost comfortable, the way old shoes are comfortable, worn into a shape that fits not because the shape is right but because your feet have given up expecting anything better. I knew the contours of abandonment. I knew exactly where it pressed and where it gave. I had been wearing it since I was five years old, since a kitchen table, since the word that was too simple for something so large.

I thought about calling my mother. I picked up the phone and set it down. What would I say? I'm sick in a foreign country and the woman I was with left? My mother would have said something practical. She would have told me to go to a hospital. She would have wired money. She would have been useful in exactly the way she was always useful, which is to say: efficiently, without touching the wound. I did not want efficiency. I wanted someone to sit with me. And asking for that, from a woman who had never been able to give it, felt like a cruelty I was not willing to inflict on either of us.

I took care of myself. That is the unsentimental truth of it. I drank the electrolyte packets. I ate what I could. I slept and woke and slept and woke. I washed my clothes in the sink because I could not find a laundromat and the effort of searching for one felt Herculean. I hung them on the balcony railing and watched them dry in the Spanish sun, my shirts and underwear pinned with clothespins alongside the neighbors' linens, becoming briefly part of the landscape of someone else's life.

---

On one of the last days, I walked to a cafe near the waterfront. A small place with tile floors and a zinc counter and three tables outside on the sidewalk. I sat down because my legs told me to.

The waiter brought bread without being asked. A basket with two thick slices, a small dish of olive oil beside it. The oil was green-gold, dense, catching the afternoon light like something alive. I tore a piece of bread and dipped it and brought it to my mouth.

The taste was so simple it almost hurt. Warm bread. Good oil. Salt. The olive oil coated my tongue and I tasted grass and pepper and something faintly bitter underneath, the way all good things carry a trace of their difficulty. My stomach, which had rejected almost everything for days, accepted this. My body, which had felt like a house someone had ransacked, found one small room still intact.

I sat at that table for an hour. I ate both slices. I ordered more bread and more oil and the waiter brought them without comment, without the sympathetic look I was bracing for, just a man doing his job, placing food in front of a woman who was hungry. The ordinariness of it was the kindest thing that had happened to me in weeks.

The sun moved across the table. The shadows of the awning shifted. People walked past on the sidewalk, their voices rising and falling in Catalan and Spanish and the occasional bright burst of English, the sound of a city that did not care about my grief, that was not interested in my story, that would continue exactly as it was whether I sat at this table or disappeared entirely.

I thought about what Nadia had said. That she wasn't ready. That she wished me well. I thought about my father, who had also not been ready. About Mark, who had been ready for everything except staying alive. About my mother, who was ready for everything except tenderness. About my grandparents, who were ready for marriage but not for the conversations that might have made it honest.

Everyone had been ready for something. Just never the thing I needed.

The olive oil pooled in the rough surface of the bread. I watched it settle into the small craters and crevices the way water finds the lowest ground, patient and without ambition, filling what was empty because that is what oil does. I thought about how the body, stripped of everything, reduced to fever and solitude and the basic animal need for fuel, will still reach for something nourishing. Will still sit down at a table and tear bread and eat.

This is what I learned in Barcelona, though I would not have called it learning at the time. At the time I would have called it surviving, which is what I called everything, because my family had taught me that surviving was the highest aspiration, the thing you did when all other options had been exhausted. But it was more than that. It was the first time I fed myself when no one was watching. The first time I sat alone with my own hunger and answered it without waiting for someone else to notice.

The sun dropped lower. The waiter collected my plate. I paid and stood and walked slowly back through the streets that Nadia had photographed and I had walked through sick and invisible, and they were still beautiful, and I was still alone, and both of those things were true at the same time, and I did not try to make one of them cancel the other.
