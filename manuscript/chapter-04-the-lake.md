# Chapter 4
## The Lake

The lake was not a lake. It was a reservoir, man-made, fed by a system of pipes and channels that someone had designed to look natural. But when you were eleven and the sun hit it right, it looked like something God put there. Flat and silver and endless, ringed by yellow hills and the dry skeletons of manzanita. Mark brought a cooler. My mother brought sunscreen and a paperback. My sister brought a towel she refused to share.

I brought nothing. I remember that specifically. I stood at the edge of the water in cutoff shorts and a swimsuit that was already too small, watching the drag boat rock gently against the dock, and I had the distinct feeling of having arrived somewhere empty-handed for something I could not yet name.

It was August. The kind of Northern California heat that flattens everything, that makes the air shimmer above the asphalt so the road looks wet when it isn't. Mark's truck had no air conditioning. We drove with the windows down and the radio loud, country music he liked and my mother tolerated, and the wind smelled like dust and gasoline and the particular sweetness of grass that has been dead for months.

Mark was already drinking in the truck.

Not obviously. Not falling-down. Just a tall can of Coors wedged between his thighs, condensation dripping onto the vinyl seat, his left hand loose on the wheel while his right hand tilted the can to his mouth at stoplights. My mother did not say anything. She had stopped saying anything about the drinking by then, or maybe she had never started. It is hard to know, looking back, which silences were strategic and which were simply exhaustion.

He was her second chance. She had left my father, or my father had left her, or they had left each other in the slow, mutual way people sometimes do. And then Mark appeared. Tall, broad-shouldered, confident in the way of men who have never been asked to question anything about themselves. He laughed loudly and drove fast and had opinions about everything. He was the opposite of my father, which I think was the point.

On the drive out, stuck in traffic on the bridge, Mark turned around in his seat and looked at me. Not the loose, unfocused look of a man already several beers in. A clear look. Present. He said, "Hey, Livvy. You know what I like about mornings like this?" I shook my head. "Nothing bad has happened yet," he said. Then he smiled. A different one. Smaller. Private. A smile that made his face look younger and sadder and more like a person than a weather system. He reached back and squeezed my knee once, briefly, the way a father would. Not my father. But a father. The kind who is right there, fully in the car, fully in the morning, and for one moment fully mine.

Three hours later his hand would be in the propeller. But on the bridge, with the sun on the water and the traffic stopped and the radio playing something I cannot remember, he was just a man who was glad nothing bad had happened yet. And I was glad too.

The lake that day was beautiful. I have to say that. Whatever came after, the morning itself was beautiful. The water was so still it looked solid, like you could walk across it. Dragonflies stitched the air above the surface. My sister lay on the dock reading a magazine, her legs dangling over the edge, her toes just touching the water. My mother sat in a folding chair under a eucalyptus tree with her book and her sunglasses, looking, from a distance, like a woman on vacation. Like a woman whose life was going well.

Mark got the boat started around noon. He had been working on it all morning, adjusting something in the engine, wiping his hands on his shorts, cracking another beer. The drag boat was his pride. A flat-bottomed thing, white with a red stripe, loud as a chainsaw when the engine caught. He had bought it used from a guy in Stockton, trailered it home himself, spent weekends in the driveway with the engine exposed, his hands black to the wrists with grease. He loved it the way some men love machines. The boat never disappointed him. The boat did not ask where he had been. The boat started when he turned the key and went where he pointed it and that, I think, was enough.

By noon he was four or five beers deep. Maybe six. I was not counting then. I learned to count later.

He took the boat out alone first, cutting wide arcs across the reservoir, the engine screaming, the wake spreading behind him in a white V that rocked the dock and made my sister's magazine pages flutter. He was showing off. For whom, I do not know. There was no one else at the lake that day. Just us. Just the four people who constituted what my mother called, with increasing strain, a family.

When he brought the boat back to the dock, he was grinning. Sweat on his forehead. His eyes had the loose, unfocused quality that I would later learn to recognize as the line between drunk and dangerous.

"Engine's running rough," he said. He said it to no one in particular, or to the boat itself, the way men sometimes talk to things they are about to break. He killed the engine and swung his legs over the side, standing knee-deep in the shallows, and then he reached for the propeller.

I was sitting on the dock. Maybe ten feet away. Close enough to see his hand move toward the blades. Close enough to see the angle of it, his thick fingers reaching under the stern of the boat the way you might reach into a drawer for something you dropped.

My mother was up by the car. My sister was in the water, floating on her back with her ears submerged, hearing nothing.

I do not know why I did not yell.

Mark's hand went into the propeller housing. He was checking something. Adjusting something. I do not know. What I know is the sound. A wet, mechanical grinding, brief, half a second at most, and then his arm jerked back and the sound he made was not a scream. It was lower than that. Animal. A sound from below language.

He stumbled backward and sat down hard in the shallow water. His right hand was out in front of him, held up like he was trying to show someone something, and the water around it turned pink, then red, then a darker red that spread outward from him in slow, terrible clouds.

For a moment nothing happened.

The dragonflies still flew. The sun still burned. My sister floated with her ears underwater, oblivious. Somewhere in the eucalyptus a bird called out twice and stopped.

Then he looked at me.

His face was white. Not pale. White, the way paper is white, the way the underside of a stone is white when you flip it in the garden. His mouth was open but the animal sound had stopped. He looked at his hand, then at me, then at his hand again.

"Livvy," he said. "Livvy, get your mom."

I ran. Barefoot on the dock, the wood hot and splintered under my feet. Up the dirt path to the parking area where my mother sat reading in her folding chair. I must have said something. I must have used words. But what I remember is the feeling of running, the slap of my feet on packed dirt, and the way my mother's face changed when she looked up.

She went white too.

---

What happened next is compressed in my memory the way car accidents are compressed for the people inside them. A series of images. The order may be wrong. The details are sharp but the sequence is uncertain, as though someone took photographs of the afternoon and shuffled them before handing them back.

My mother at the water's edge. Mark sitting in the shallows, cradling his hand against his chest, the front of his shirt soaked dark. My sister standing up in the water, magazine forgotten, her face a mask of something between confusion and horror.

Towels. My mother wrapping his hand in a beach towel, the terrycloth going red immediately, the way a napkin absorbs a spill, quick and total. Mark not crying, not screaming, just breathing hard through his nose and saying "It's fine, it's fine, it's fine," which it was not.

The walk to the truck. Mark leaning on my mother. His weight against her. She was not a large woman. Five foot four, a hundred and twenty pounds. She held him up.

Then the question.

I heard it from the backseat where I was already sitting, my sister beside me, our legs still wet, sand stuck to our shins. Mark was in the passenger seat, his wrapped hand pressed against his stomach, his head tilted back, breathing through his mouth. My mother stood at the driver's side door with the keys in her hand.

She could not drive stick.

Mark's truck was a manual. A 1994 Ford Ranger with a five-speed and a clutch that stuck. My mother drove an automatic. She had always driven an automatic. She stood there with the keys and I watched her face do the math, watched the options narrow, watched the moment she understood.

She turned around and looked at me.

"Olivia," she said. "Can you drive?"

I was eleven years old. I had never driven a car. I had sat on Mark's lap once and steered the truck down a dirt road, which is not driving, which is a game adults play with children that teaches them nothing useful about what happens when you are alone behind the wheel and someone is bleeding.

"I don't know how," I said.

"I'll tell you what to do," she said. "Mark will tell you the gears."

But at the time I just climbed into the driver's seat.

I was too small. My feet barely reached the pedals. Mark, from the passenger seat, his voice tight and strange, told me to push the seat forward. I found the lever and slid it up until my toes could press the clutch to the floor. The seat was still warm from him. The steering wheel smelled like beer and sunscreen.

"Turn the key," my mother said from the backseat.

I turned the key. The engine caught. The truck vibrated beneath me, and the vibration went up through the seat and into my body and I felt every part of the machine at once, the way it wanted to move, the way it was waiting to be told what to do.

"First gear," Mark said. His voice was quiet now. Controlled. As though the emergency had made him temporarily sober, or at least temporarily capable of the performance of sobriety. "Push the clutch in. Move the stick to the left and up."

I pushed the clutch. I moved the stick. It resisted, then gave, sliding into place with a metallic click.

"Let the clutch out slow."

I let the clutch out too fast. The truck lurched forward and stalled. Mark made a sound through his teeth.

"Slower," he said.

The second time, I got it. The truck crept forward across the gravel lot, and I turned the wheel toward the road, and the tires found the asphalt, and then we were moving.

The road from the reservoir to the nearest hospital was fourteen miles. I know this because I have looked it up since. At the time it felt like a hundred. Two lanes, no shoulder, dry golden hills on either side, the occasional oak tree throwing a shadow across the road. I drove with both hands gripping the wheel at ten and two the way they teach you in driver's ed, which I would not take for another five years. My knuckles were white. The sun was directly ahead of us, low enough to make me squint but not low enough to block. The windshield was filthy, streaked with dust and the dried remains of insects, and the light came through it diffused and golden, almost pretty, which felt obscene.

Mark told me when to shift. Second gear at twenty. Third at thirty. I never made it past third. Thirty miles an hour felt like flying. The truck drifted toward the center line and I corrected, overcorrected, felt the gravel on the shoulder bite under the right tires and pulled back. My heart was in my throat. My jaw ached from clenching.

In the rearview mirror I could see my mother. Her arms around my sister. My sister crying. My mother's face perfectly still, watching the road over my shoulder, watching me, her eleven-year-old daughter, drive her bleeding husband to the hospital in his own truck.

A car passed us going the other direction. The driver honked. Maybe because I was driving too slowly. Maybe because he could see I was a child. I do not know. The sound made me flinch and the truck swerved and Mark said, "You're doing fine, Livvy. Keep going. You're doing great."

He was lying. I was not doing great. My hands were shaking so badly the steering wheel vibrated between them. My right foot trembled on the gas pedal. I was eleven years old and I was driving a manual transmission truck on a two-lane highway with a drunk man bleeding through a towel beside me, and I was not doing great.

But I kept going.

The hospital appeared like something from a dream. A low beige building on the right side of the road with a red emergency sign and a parking lot half full of cars driven there by adults. I pulled in too fast, hit the curb, stalled the engine. It did not matter. We were there.

After that, the adults took over.

Nurses in scrubs. A wheelchair. Mark making jokes already, something about how he should have let the fish win, which no one laughed at. The bright, buzzing hallway. The smell of antiseptic and floor wax and the particular staleness of air that has been recycled through a building designed to keep people alive.

My mother filled out forms at the front desk. My sister and I sat in plastic chairs in the waiting room. The fluorescent lights above us hummed at a frequency I could feel in my fillings. The walls were the color of old teeth. A television mounted in the corner played a talk show with the volume too low to hear, just mouths moving, people smiling about nothing.

I sat very still.

My sister leaned against me and fell asleep. Her hair smelled like lake water and sunscreen. Her breathing evened out, slow and trusting, the way only a child can sleep in a place like that, in the middle of a day like that, as though the crisis were already over simply because someone else was in charge.

I did not sleep. I sat with my hands in my lap and stared at the fluorescent lights and I did not move for two hours. A nurse brought me a juice box at some point. Apple. I drank it without tasting it.

Mark came out with his hand wrapped in white gauze, thick and professional, holding it slightly elevated the way the doctor must have told him to. He was smiling. My mother walked beside him, carrying his discharge papers, her face arranged in the particular expression she used when she needed everyone to believe things were fine.

"Good as new," Mark said, holding up the bandaged hand like a trophy. "Couple stitches. No big deal."

Couple stitches. No big deal.

We drove home in my mother's car, which she had apparently called a neighbor to bring to the hospital while I sat in the waiting room counting ceiling tiles. I sat in the backseat again. Where I belonged. Where children sit. The vinyl was cool against the backs of my legs and the car smelled like air freshener, pine-scented, artificial, and I pressed my forehead against the window and watched the hills go by and felt the strange hollowing that comes after adrenaline drains out of you, the body suddenly light and empty, a cup someone has poured out.

We stopped for pizza on the way home and Mark ate with his left hand and made everyone laugh and my mother drank a glass of wine and my sister got pepperoni on her shirt and the evening closed around us the way evenings do, ordinary and warm, as though nothing had happened.

That night I lay in bed and held my hands up in the dark and looked at them. They were still shaking. Small hands. A child's hands, with chipped nail polish and a friendship bracelet my sister had made me, the threads already fraying. I held them above me and watched them tremble against the ceiling, and I thought about how they had held a steering wheel, how they had turned a key, how they had done something no one had ever taught them to do.

I did not feel proud.

---

The lake appears on our right, sudden and flat and silver, and my stomach drops.

A reservoir. Man-made. Some government project from the seventies, framed now by October hills gone the color of bread crust. The water catches the late morning sun and sends it back in a glare so bright I look away.

My mother is driving. I am in the passenger seat. My sister is in the back with her phone. We are twenty minutes from the venue, maybe less. I am getting married today and the road is smooth and my mother drives an automatic and no one is bleeding and I am thirty-four years old.

I count the mile markers. Eleven. Twelve. Thirteen. My fingers tap the armrest in groups of four. Four taps, pause. Four taps, pause. It used to be three. At fifteen, three was the number the universe required. Somewhere along the way, three stopped being enough. The body does not outgrow anything. It just revises its terms.

I check the time on my phone. 10:47 a.m. The ceremony is at 2:00. That is one hundred and ninety-three minutes. I divide this by manageable intervals. Forty-eight sets of four, with one left over. The remainder bothers me. I recalculate. The math is soothing. The math is always soothing. It does not matter what the numbers mean. It matters that they hold still.

A photo arrives from Will. His shoes, polished, dark brown, sitting neatly beside his suit on a hotel bed. *Ready when you are.*

I stare at the photo. The shoes placed carefully, toes pointing the same direction. The suit laid flat without a wrinkle. The quiet order of a man preparing to wait for someone. The thought of it, of someone choosing to wait, still feels foreign. Like a language I am learning late.

The lake disappears behind a hill.

The road curves. Vineyards appear on both sides, rows of vines stripped nearly bare for autumn, their posts and wires casting thin shadows on the dirt. And then, on a slope just above the venue's gravel drive, olive trees. A small grove of them, their trunks twisted and silvery, their leaves catching the light and releasing it, catching and releasing, patient as breathing.

My mother slows the car and turns into the drive.

My hands are on my lap now. Fingers laced. Still.
