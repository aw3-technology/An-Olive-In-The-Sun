# Chapter 7
## The Uniform

I see her before she sees me.

She is crossing the gravel lot toward the ceremony area, one hand holding down the hem of a sundress the wind keeps catching, the other gripping a clutch purse the color of ballet slippers. Her hair is different. Shorter, darker, blown straight in a way that makes her jaw look sharper than I remember. But her walk is the same. That slight forward lean, as if she is always arriving somewhere just ahead of schedule.

Caroline Reyes. Eighth grade. Sister Mary Catherine's homeroom. A lifetime ago.

Guests are filtering in now. The venue is filling with voices and the particular rustle of people in formal clothing trying to look at ease. A woman I have not seen since college kisses my cheek and tells me I look radiant. A man whose name I cannot place shakes my hand and says he always knew Will would settle down. I smile. I thank him. I say something about being so glad they could come. The words come easily because they require nothing of me. They are the verbal equivalent of the programs stacked on the welcome table, printed and laminated and utterly beside the point.

I adjust my champagne glass on the cocktail table. Move it a quarter inch to the left. Then back. Then slightly forward, so the base aligns with the edge of the napkin. An intrusive thought arrives without knocking: what if I trip walking down the aisle? I push it away. It comes back wearing different clothes. What if I trip and the dress tears? I push it under again and take a sip of champagne I do not taste.

Caroline spots me across the patio. She raises a hand.

And just like that, I am thirteen years old.

---

The uniform was a plaid skirt in green and navy, hemmed exactly to the knee. White button-down. Green cardigan or navy blazer depending on the season. Knee socks. Black shoes, no logos. A small gold crucifix on a chain, optional but conspicuous in its absence if you chose not to wear it.

I wore the crucifix.

Not because I believed, exactly. Because the girls who didn't wear it were making a statement, and I was not yet ready to make statements. I was thirteen and recently deposited into an all-girls Catholic school by a mother who believed structure might do for me what she could not. Which was hold me in place.

She was not wrong.

The school sat on a hill above a parking lot shaded by live oaks, a two-story stucco building with arched windows and a chapel that smelled like beeswax and old hymnals. Morning assembly in the courtyard. The Pledge of Allegiance followed by a Hail Mary. Homeroom, then six periods, then dismissal at three fifteen. Every day mapped the same way, every girl dressed the same way, every hour accounted for. After the chaos of my mother's house, after Mark and the go-kart and the hospital and the silences that followed his episodes, the repetition felt less like confinement than relief.

There is a particular freedom in being told what to wear. You wake up and the question is already answered. The plaid skirt, the white shirt, the cardigan. You look like everyone else. For a girl who spent most of her time scanning rooms for danger, reading faces for the first sign of collapse, the sameness was a kind of rest. I could stop performing. I could just be a girl in a uniform, indistinguishable from forty other girls in the same uniform, and the anonymity was the closest thing to safety I had ever known.

I found Megan first. Or she found me. It is hard to remember the mechanics of early friendship because it happens the way weather happens: gradually, and then all at once, and by the time you notice, you are already inside it.

Megan Callahan sat behind me in Sister Mary Catherine's religion class and passed me a note during a lecture on the Beatitudes that said, in round, careful handwriting: *Is it just me or does Sister MC look exactly like a parrot?*

She did, in fact, look exactly like a parrot. The curved nose. The darting head movements. The green cardigan she wore every single day regardless of season.

I turned around and Megan was already grinning, and I knew. The way you know at thirteen, instantly and without evidence, that someone is your person.

Caroline came next, pulled into our orbit by proximity and a shared lunch period. She was quieter than Megan, more watchful. She carried a worn copy of *The Perks of Being a Wallflower* in her backpack for the entire first semester, its spine so cracked the pages were starting to separate. She read it the way other girls read Scripture. I understood that impulse. Some books are not books. They are mirrors shaped like paper.

By October the three of us were inseparable in the way that only teenage girls can be inseparable: violently, desperately, as if the friendship itself were a country we had founded and would defend with our lives. We ate lunch together on the bench near the chapel garden, trading halves of sandwiches and complaining about the same teachers with the reverence of soldiers cursing their commanding officers. We passed notes folded into elaborate origami shapes, triangles and stars and once a tiny paper crane that Megan made during a study hall on the Eucharist. We had a language. Not a code, exactly. A frequency. Inside jokes that had compressed over so many repetitions they were now single words, sounds, a raised eyebrow that could contain an entire conversation.

"Parrot" meant Sister Mary Catherine was watching.

"Tuesday" meant one of us was having a bad day and needed the other two to close ranks without asking why.

"Lighthouse" meant someone had a crush, though we never specified on whom, which was its own kind of protection.

It was Megan who said it first, although saying it makes it sound more deliberate than it was. We were sitting on her bedroom floor one Saturday afternoon, painting our toenails a shade of pink her mother had deemed appropriate and we had deemed tragic. The Blackberry sitting between us on the carpet, its green notification light blinking every few minutes with a message from someone who was not in the room and therefore did not matter.

"Do you ever feel like you're pretending to be a person?" Megan said. She was not looking at me. She was looking at her toes.

"All the time," I said.

"Not like lying," she said. "Like, the person everyone sees. The one in the uniform. She's not the real one."

Caroline was sitting cross-legged by the closet, braiding a friendship bracelet she would never finish. She looked up and said nothing, which was her way of saying everything.

The room was quiet except for the hum of Megan's ceiling fan and something playing softly from the Blackberry's speaker, tinny and distant. I remember the light in that room. Saturday afternoon light, golden, unhurried, slanting through the blinds in long parallel lines across the carpet. The kind of light that makes a room feel held.

I wanted to tell them things. About Mark. About the propeller and the hospital and the way my mother's face looked when she thought no one was watching. About the feeling I carried in my body like a second skeleton, this rigid awareness that the world could shift beneath you at any moment and there was nothing to hold onto.

But I didn't.

What I did instead was lean my head against Megan's bedframe and close my eyes and listen to them breathe. And that was enough. Proximity was its own confession. We did not need to name the damage to know it was there.

At night, after my mother's house went quiet, I lay in bed with the Blackberry under my pillow, its green light pulsing softly against the pillowcase. A small heartbeat. A proof of connection. Megan or Caroline or both of them out there somewhere in the dark, awake or asleep, existing. The phone was a tether, a thin wire strung between my bed and theirs, and as long as that light pulsed, I was not alone in the particular way that terrified me most.

Sometimes a message would come through at eleven, twelve, one in the morning. Never anything important.

*you up?*

*can't sleep*

*tuesday*

Just the words. Just the knowing that someone was out there holding the other end.

There were things I noticed and did not name. The way Megan's hand sometimes rested on my arm a beat too long. The way I watched Caroline's mouth when she talked and felt something I filed under "friendship" because I did not have another word for it yet. The way the three of us existed in a world without boys, and the absence of boys created a space where tenderness between girls was permissible, even expected, and I could not always tell where expected ended and something else began.

I did not think about this. I thought around it, the way you walk around a hole in the floor. Aware. Careful. Not ready.

The uniform helped. When everyone looks the same, desire has nowhere to attach. Or it attaches everywhere, diffused and ambient, a low hum beneath the plaid and the prayers and the Hail Marys. You could love a girl in a way the school had no language for, and the school's silence on the subject functioned as both prohibition and permission. What goes unnamed goes unpoliced.

I loved Megan and Caroline the way you love the only country you have ever known. Completely. Without perspective.

I did not know it was the kind of love that could be lost.

---

Megan's parents told her in January. The family was moving to Portland. Her father had taken a job at a firm up there, something in consulting that required relocation and apparently could not be negotiated. Megan delivered the news at lunch on a Tuesday, which felt like a violation of our own code. Tuesdays were for silent solidarity, not demolition.

She was crying. Not the loud, uncomplicated crying of a thirteen-year-old, but the restrained, angry crying of someone who has been told that her grief is secondary to a logistics problem.

"It's not even my choice," she said, and the sentence landed in my chest like a stone.

I wanted to say something large enough to contain what I felt. What I said was: "When?"

"March."

Two months. I counted them the way I counted everything. Sixty-one days. Eight and a half weeks. Roughly forty-three school days, minus holidays and weekends. I could map the loss in calendar squares before I could feel it.

Caroline was quiet. She put her hand on Megan's knee and left it there.

Megan transferred in March, and the school shrank around her absence. Her desk in religion class sat empty for a week before a new girl filled it, a girl named Sarah who had perfectly straight hair and no interest in origami. The bench by the chapel garden became two-thirds of what it had been, and two-thirds is a fraction that refuses to feel whole.

Then, in April, Caroline.

Her mother had decided to enroll her in the public school across town. Something about the tuition being unsustainable, about a scholarship that had not come through, about practical considerations that Caroline relayed with the flat affect of someone reading a verdict. She told me after school on a Thursday, standing by the pickup loop, her backpack hanging from one shoulder.

"My mom says it doesn't make sense anymore."

I nodded. I understood about things not making sense anymore.

"I'll still see you," she said. "On weekends."

We both knew that weekends were where friendships went to die. Weekends were a hospice for the kind of closeness that required hallways and shared desks and the daily accumulation of presence. You cannot sustain a country through occasional visits. The borders dissolve. The language fades.

Caroline left in May.

And then it was just me. Me and the uniform and the empty bench and a school that had not changed at all except for the fact that it had changed completely.

I sat through classes. I ate lunch alone, or with girls who were kind in the vague, unthreatening way of people who do not need you but are willing to share a table. I walked the halls in my plaid skirt, and the skirt that had once felt like belonging now felt like a costume. The anonymity I had loved became invisibility. The structure became a cage.

The notes stopped. The green light still pulsed under my pillow at night, but less frequently, and the messages that came were polite and distant, the verbal equivalent of a wave from across a parking lot. *How's school? Miss you! We should hang out sometime!* The exclamation points doing the work that intimacy used to do.

I lasted until June.

I did not tell my mother I wanted to leave. I told her I needed to leave. The distinction was important, though she did not seem to register it. She was dealing with Mark again, with whatever the current crisis was, and my school situation occupied a lower shelf of her attention. She asked if I was sure. I said yes. She signed the transfer paperwork at the kitchen table between two phone calls, one to the insurance company and one to a lawyer, and that was it.

The last day was a Friday. The campus already had the loosened feel of approaching summer, girls in their uniforms but with the sleeves rolled up, the cardigans abandoned, the rules softening as the year wound down.

I walked to my locker. Emptied it. Textbooks, notebooks, a photo of the three of us taped to the inside of the door that I peeled off carefully and slipped into my backpack. A tube of strawberry lip gloss I had borrowed from Megan and never returned.

I walked down the hall. Through the double doors. Down the front steps.

The parking lot. The live oaks. The heat rising off the blacktop.

I got into the car. My mother did not ask how it went. She was on the phone.

That evening I stood in my bedroom and took off the uniform for the last time. Unbuttoned the white shirt. Unzipped the plaid skirt. Stepped out of it the way you step out of a room you know you will never enter again. Hung everything on a hanger in the back of the closet, where it would stay untouched until my mother donated it to Goodwill the following September without asking.

I stood there in my underwear and looked at the closet. My actual clothes. Jeans, t-shirts, a denim jacket, things I had chosen but that suddenly felt arbitrary. The uniform had answered the question of who I was every morning for two years. Now the question was mine, and I had no idea what the answer was.

I lay on my bed and stared at the ceiling and felt the particular emptiness of a person standing in a doorway. Behind me, a world of plaid skirts and chapel gardens and two girls who had made me feel like I belonged to something I had chosen rather than inherited. Ahead of me, nothing I could name. A public school full of strangers. A closet full of clothes that would not tell me who to be.

The Blackberry sat on my nightstand. The green light was dark.

No one was sending anything. No one was awake on the other end, holding the wire. The tether had come loose so slowly I had not noticed until it was already gone.

I pulled the covers over my head and lay there in the dark, breathing. Not crying. Not sleeping. Just existing in the space between what I had lost and what I had not yet found. The first fracture of belonging beyond family. The first time I understood that even the things you choose can be taken from you.

---

Caroline is walking toward me now, across the patio, past the cocktail tables and the arriving guests and the string lights that the venue staff are draping along the fence posts. She is smiling. She looks like someone who grew up and did it gracefully, without the detours.

She reaches me and we hug. The embrace is warm and brief, the kind of hug you give someone you once knew completely and now know not at all. Her perfume smells like gardenias. She holds me at arm's length and says I look beautiful.

"Thank you for coming," I say, and mean it, though what I mean is more complicated than gratitude.

She starts telling me about her life. Two kids. A house in Walnut Creek. She teaches second grade. She says it like a list, the way people summarize decades when standing on patios at weddings. I nod and smile and ask the right questions, and somewhere beneath the conversation I am watching a girl in a plaid skirt fold a note into a paper crane, and another girl with a cracked paperback in her bag, and a bedroom with Saturday light striped across the carpet.

A version of me that no longer exists, known by a person who no longer knows me.

I watch Caroline rejoin her husband by the bar, and I hold my champagne glass and wonder what she would say if I told her the truth. That the uniform was the last thing I ever wore that told me exactly who to be. That I have been standing in front of the closet ever since.
