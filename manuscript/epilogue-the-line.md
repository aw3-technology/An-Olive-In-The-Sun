# Epilogue
## The Line

The hotel room is quiet. The same room I woke in this morning, the same thin curtains, the same framed watercolor of a pear that looks nothing like a pear. My shoes are by the door. My dress is hanging on the closet where it started, except now the hem is stained and the lace at the shoulder is torn where someone stepped on it during the last dance and I laughed instead of flinching, which felt like a small victory over every version of myself that came before.

Will is asleep. I can hear him breathing in the other room, the low, even rhythm of a man who falls asleep in minutes, which I have never understood and have decided to stop resenting.

I am sitting on the edge of the bed in the dark, the way I sat here fourteen hours ago, except now the terror has a different shape. Quieter. Less like a fist and more like a current, something running beneath the surface, present but not in charge.

The fear did not disappear. I want to be honest about that. I stood at the altar and said the words and meant them and the fear was there the whole time, sitting in the fourth row like an uninvited guest, watching.

It will always be there. I know this now. The counting, the checking, the small rituals my hands perform without permission. The voice in the back of my skull that says: this is the part where it falls apart. That voice is not going away. It is the voice of a five-year-old at a kitchen table, and she has been talking for twenty-nine years, and she has earned the right to speak.

But she is not the only voice anymore.

I think about the women who came before me. My grandmother, who stayed married for forty years and never once, as far as I know, said the word "unhappy" out loud. Who endured in a silence so total it became the air she breathed. Who taught her daughter that a woman's job was to hold the house together and never mention the weight.

My mother, who survived differently. Who worked. Who woke every morning and put on the suit and managed other people's money and other people's schedules and other people's crises while her own life burned behind her. Who held things together through willpower and routine and a glass of wine at the end of the day that became two. Who never fell apart. Who never, not once, sat down and said: this is what happened to me, and this is what it cost.

And me. The one who fell apart. The one who landed in a hospital bed and was given a name for the thing she carried. The one who broke down and rebuilt and broke down again and rebuilt again, not because she was stronger but because she had no other choice, because the alternative was the silence her grandmother chose or the willpower her mother chose, and neither of those fit the shape of her life.

Three generations. Three ways of surviving. Silence. Endurance. Collapse and reconstruction.

The line runs through us like a wire. I can feel it in my body, the inheritance I did not ask for. The anxiety that hums beneath my ribs. The way I check the lock. The way I flinch, still, when love gets too close too fast. I will pass some of this on whether I mean to or not. I know that. The patterns live in the nervous system, not in the conscious mind, and they will slip through the cracks of my best intentions and show up in the next generation with a different face and the same architecture.

But I also know what I did not receive. No one in my family ever sat down and said: this is what happened to us. This is the shape of the thing we carry. This is what it cost your grandmother, and this is what it cost your mother, and this is what it cost me. And this is what I am choosing differently.

No one named the line.

I am naming it now. Or trying to. Sitting in the dark in a hotel room on my wedding night, I am trying to trace the shape of the thing I carry, and I cannot see it whole. I can feel the current. I can feel the pills on the nightstand and the therapist's card in my wallet and the man breathing in the other room who stayed. I can feel the choices I have made, lined up beside the choices I inherited, and I cannot tell you where one set ends and the other begins. I do not know if naming the line breaks it. I do not know if choosing differently is enough to change the architecture, or if the wire runs too deep, woven into the nervous system, the chemistry, the marrow. I do not know if this marriage will hold. I do not know if I will be the kind of mother, if I become one, who sits down at the kitchen table and says the true thing, or if I will discover, when the moment arrives, that silence is easier than I thought and my mother's face is waiting for me in the mirror.

I do not know.

I chose this anyway. I am choosing it now, in the dark, with the fear still humming and the counting still ticking and no guarantee that the pattern ends with me. I chose it not because I solved the question but because I finally understood that the question does not have a solution. It has a practice. A daily, ordinary, uncinematic practice of waking up and choosing again.

Outside the window, the first light is coming. Not yet sunrise. Just the earliest suggestion of it, the sky shifting from black to a deep, uncertain blue, the kind of light that is not quite anything yet. Not day. Not night. Something between.

Northern California in October. The same sun that woke me fourteen hours ago. The same sun that will ask the same question tomorrow.

I stand up. I walk to the window and I open the curtains.

The light finds me. I do not know what it will show.

I let it.
