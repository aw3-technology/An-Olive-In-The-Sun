# Chapter 8
## Public School

A woman in a green silk dress is telling her husband about her twenty-year reunion. She holds a champagne flute in one hand and gestures with the other, painting the scene. The gymnasium. The streamers. How they turned the old cafeteria into a cocktail lounge, how strange it was to drink Pinot Grigio in the room where they once ate square pizza on Styrofoam trays. She names people: Jamie Kowalski, who became a dentist. Megan Ferris, who married their AP History teacher. Can you imagine? Her husband shakes his head. She laughs. The laugh of a woman whose past is a warm room she can walk into whenever she wants.

I am standing close enough to hear this because I am pretending to examine the place cards on the table beside them. Rearranging them, actually. Straightening the ones that are slightly crooked, aligning each card with the edge of its plate. Four cards adjusted. I stop myself at four.

She describes driving past her old high school on the way to the event, how the marquee still had the same font. She tells him she sat in the parking lot for a minute. Just sat there, in the car, looking at the building where she became herself. The casualness of it. The easy ownership of a place, a class of '04, a homeroom she could still name by number. The assumption that everyone carries something like this. A school. A locker. A lunch table that was theirs.

---

I was fifteen the first time I walked into a public school.

The building smelled like floor wax and body spray and something underneath both, something institutional and warm, like the air itself had been circulated through too many lungs. The hallway stretched in both directions, lined with orange lockers, and the fluorescent lights hummed at a frequency I could feel behind my teeth.

I had spent the last two years in plaid skirts. Knee socks. A white collared blouse buttoned to the second button, because Sister Bernadette checked, and saddle shoes that squeaked against the polished chapel floor. Catholic school had rules for everything. How to stand. How to sit. How to fold your hands during prayer so the left thumb crossed over the right, because the right side was the side of God and needed to be closer to your heart. I hadn't believed any of it, but I'd understood the architecture. There was comfort in being told exactly who to be.

My two best friends had transferred out the year before. Both of them, within months of each other, pulled into new zip codes by parental decisions that had nothing to do with us and everything to do with the quiet rearrangements adults make when their own lives shift. I followed. Not to their schools. Just away from the one that no longer held me.

So I stood in the hallway of a public school in an outfit I had assembled that morning with the seriousness of someone defusing a bomb. Low-rise jeans. A fitted top from PacSun that I thought said casual but probably said trying. Vans I had bought the week before and scuffed deliberately against the sidewalk to make them look less new.

I knew within thirty seconds that I had gotten it wrong. Not catastrophically. Just enough. The jeans were the wrong wash. The top was a season behind. The Vans were fine but everyone here wore Etnies, a brand I had never heard of, and the difference between Vans and Etnies was apparently the difference between understanding something and understanding nothing.

A girl with straightened hair and a Juicy Couture hoodie looked at me, then looked away, and in that single motion communicated a verdict so complete it did not require a word.

The cafeteria was worse. I stood in the doorway holding a brown paper lunch bag my mother had packed, which marked me as someone whose mother still packed lunches, which marked me as someone who did not yet know that buying a soft pretzel from the snack bar was the minimum price of social viability. The room was enormous. Round tables, not long ones, and the seating was a language I could not read. Who sat where and why. Which table was the theater kids, which was the athletes, which was the cluster of boys in oversized hoodies who watched everyone else with the performative boredom of people who had decided, at fifteen, that nothing was worth their enthusiasm.

Boys. That was the other thing. At the all-girls school, boys had been theoretical. Discussed during lunch in the courtyard, analyzed through song lyrics and rumors, imagined in the abstract. Here they were physical. Loud. They moved through the hallways in packs, their voices cracking and recovering, their bodies taking up space with a confidence that seemed to come from nowhere and require nothing. They shoved each other against lockers and called it affection. They yelled across the cafeteria. One of them, tall, in a beanie, caught me staring and grinned, and I looked at the floor so fast I nearly tripped.

I sat at an empty table near the window. Ate my sandwich. Did not look up.

By the end of the first week, I had mapped the room. Not consciously. The way you learn the exits in a building without deciding to, the way your eyes find the door before your mind admits it is looking. This table is safe. That group will let you sit on the edge without asking questions. That boy by the vending machine watches everyone who walks past, and you should not walk past. The girl in the corner with the headphones is alone on purpose, which is different from being alone by accident, and if you sit near her she will not speak to you but she will not move, and that is enough.

I learned to read a room in thirty seconds. Who was dangerous, who was bored, who was watching, who would let you disappear. It was a skill I had been practicing since I was five years old, sitting at the kitchen table while my parents' marriage ended, and I did not yet know it had a clinical name. I just thought I was paying attention.

---

The problem was not one school. It was the number of them.

I changed schools three times between sophomore and senior year. Twice for moves, once because a new stepfather brought a new school district the way he brought everything else: without warning and without consultation. Each transfer erased whatever fragile social progress I had managed at the last one. Each first day was the same equation: wrong outfit, wrong references, wrong everything, recalibrated on the fly with the frantic efficiency of someone who has learned that belonging is a performance you must rehearse from scratch every time the stage changes.

At the second school, I made a friend named Casey who wore a Taking Back Sunday hoodie and drove a Honda Civic with a cracked windshield. We ate lunch in her car for three weeks. Then my mother told me we were moving again. I remember Casey's face when I said I was leaving. Not surprise. Something flatter than that. She had the expression of someone being told about weather: noted, absorbed, incorporated into the forecast without protest. She was used to people leaving. So was I. We did not exchange numbers. We both knew better.

At the third school, I stopped trying to make friends in the first month. I sat through classes and took notes and left. The hallways were just hallways. The cafeteria was just a room where I ate quickly and alone, my headphones in, Jesse Lacey's voice threading through the noise like a second, more honest narration of my life.

I was becoming an expert in arrival. Walking into a new room, a new school, a new set of faces with their own inside jokes and histories I would never share. Each time, the same silent assessment. Who is safe. Where do I sit. How long until I leave.

The growing certainty, settling into my body like weather, was this: I would never be from anywhere. Other people had hometowns. High schools they could name with the warm, possessive tone of the woman in the green dress. I had a series of buildings I passed through, leaving nothing behind, taking nothing with me except the ability to scan a room and find the safest corner.

---

My bedroom was the only room that stayed.

Not the same bedroom. The furniture changed, the addresses changed, the view from the window changed. But the interior remained mine. I rebuilt it every time, the way some people rebuild temples after a flood. The same objects, the same arrangement, the same careful architecture of identity reassembled on whatever new floor I was given.

Converse by the door, laces untied. Magazine clippings on the wall: pages torn from Nylon and SPIN and a Delia's catalogue I kept for the aesthetic, not the clothes. Band posters held up with thumbtacks that left constellations of small holes wherever we went. Dried flowers in a mason jar on the dresser, lavender and baby's breath I bought at a farmers market and kept long past the point when they crumbled at the stem. A candle on the nightstand, sometimes two. Vanilla, always vanilla, because the smell was the first thing I controlled in any new space, and vanilla smelled like a decision I had made.

Brand New through the speakers. Deja Entendu on repeat, the astronaut floating alone on the cover, untethered and calm. I lay on my bed and listened to Jesse Lacey sing about the things he could not fix, and I thought: someone understands. Not me, specifically. But the frequency. The particular hum of being young and sad and unable to explain why the sadness felt so much bigger than your life warranted.

Blink-182 for driving. Taking Back Sunday for anger. Rilo Kiley for the nights when the sadness turned philosophical and Jenny Lewis made loneliness sound like something you could study rather than something that studied you. The Decemberists for pretending I was somewhere else entirely, somewhere with shipwrecks and lanterns and a vocabulary that had nothing to do with cafeterias or custody arrangements.

The Jetta came later. Used, silver, a dent in the passenger door from a parking lot I never identified. I covered the bumper in stickers: bands, a peace sign, a quote I cannot remember now but believed at the time was the most important sentence ever written. I smoked Parliaments with the windows down, the recessed filter elegant between my fingers, the smoke curling out into whatever suburb I was driving through. The car smelled like cigarettes and vanilla air freshener and the particular staleness of a vehicle that served as a living room, a confessional, and an escape pod.

I started wearing only Converse and Vans. Band tees I found at thrift stores. Lace and satin layered over cotton, the beginnings of an aesthetic I would spend years refining: shabby and deliberate, pretty and damaged, soft things worn until they frayed. My clothes were the only constant. I could walk into any school wearing the same Converse and feel, underneath the wrong jeans and the wrong references, that I was still the same person. Whoever that was.

Facebook arrived like a second life. I created a profile the way a novelist creates a character: selectively, strategically, assembling the version of myself I wanted to exist. The profile photo angled just right. The interests curated. The wall posts calibrated to suggest a person who was thoughtful and a little dark and listening to better music than you. I posted Brand New lyrics as my status. Lines about sinking and quiet houses and the weight of things unspoken. Coded transmissions, broadcast to no one in particular, hoping someone would hear the frequency and respond.

No one did. Or they did, but only with a like, a thumbs-up, the digital equivalent of a nod across a crowded room from someone who recognized the song but not the cry inside it.

I scrolled through other people's profiles late at night, studying the evidence of their lives: group photos at football games, inside jokes in comment threads, albums titled "BEST FRIENDS FOREVER" in all capitals. The documentation of belonging. I had nothing to document. My photo albums were empty or curated to suggest a life more populated than it was. A picture of the Jetta at sunset. A close-up of my Converse on a curb. The aesthetic of solitude repackaged as taste.

The pink walls of childhood were behind me. My new rooms were blue. Navy comforters. Dusty blue paint I chose myself at the third apartment. The color of distance. Of something cooling. I did not mourn the pink. It had never been mine. It had been assigned to me the way the plaid skirt had been assigned to me, a girlhood someone else designed. Blue was the first color I chose, and choosing it felt like the first true sentence I had ever spoken.

---

The woman in the green dress is finishing her reunion story. Her husband refills her champagne. She mentions that her best friend from sophomore year still lives two blocks from the school, still drives past it every morning on the way to the elementary school where she teaches. She says this with a kind of wonder, as though the continuity itself is remarkable, as though staying in one place your whole life is an achievement rather than just a thing that happened because nothing forced you to leave.

I smile. A small, polite smile, the kind I have been practicing since the first cafeteria, the first new hallway, the first morning of standing in a doorway and calculating where to sit. The smile of a woman who learned, very young, that arrival is not the same as belonging, and that some people build their lives in a single place while others build theirs from whatever room they happen to be standing in.

Through a window, I catch a glimpse of Will across the venue, leaning against a doorframe, talking to his best man. He went to one school. K through 12, the same brick building, the same friends, the same lunch table for thirteen years. He describes his childhood the way the woman in green describes her reunion: warmly, simply, without the awareness that what he is describing is extraordinary. He does not know what it cost me to get here. Not because he does not care. Because some distances can only be measured by someone who walked them.

The woman laughs at something her husband says. I reach for a champagne glass on the table. The bubbles rise in thin columns, small and persistent, each one arriving at the surface and vanishing the instant it gets there.

I have been arriving somewhere new my entire life. The only thing I ever brought with me was the ability to leave.
