# Chapter 10
## Hollywood

A woman I don't recognize crosses the lawn in heels that should be impossible on grass. She moves like someone who has solved this problem before, weight forward, balls of her feet, each step a small decision. Gold dress. Hair that has been touched by at least three people today. She air-kisses Ethan's aunt and takes a glass of champagne from a passing tray without breaking stride. I watch her the way you watch a nature documentary. The effortlessness is the performance, and the performance is flawless.

Someone beside me, a friend of Ethan's mother, leans in. "You used to model, didn't you?"

I turn. She is smiling. It is a generous smile, the kind attached to a compliment that hasn't quite arrived yet.

"You should have stayed in it. You could have been big." She touches my arm lightly, a punctuation mark. Her eyes move across my face, my collarbone, the line of my dress, quick as a cash register.

"Thank you," I say. And I do mean it. I am grateful for the kindness inside the sentence, even as the sentence itself lands somewhere I did not invite it. Being told you should have stayed in modeling is a specific kind of compliment. It praises the thing you were and implies the thing you became was less. She does not know this. She is being nice. I smile and tilt my head the way I learned to tilt it a long time ago, and the muscle memory is so precise it frightens me.

---

The first person who told me I could model was a woman at a coffee shop in West Hollywood. I was twenty-two, maybe six months out of college, working a hostess job at a restaurant on Melrose while I figured out what a philosophy degree was actually for. She handed me a card with a name on it and an address on La Brea and said, "You should come in." As if the agency were a party. As if I'd been personally invited.

I went. Of course I went.

The office was on the second floor above a vintage clothing store, and the stairwell smelled like dust and someone's perfume. Inside, everything was white. White walls, white desk, white leather chairs. A girl about my age sat in one of them, flipping through a portfolio. She didn't look up. On the wall behind the receptionist were composites: rows of faces arranged in a grid, each one reduced to its best angle.

They measured me. That is what they did first. A woman with a tape measure put her hands on my hips like she was adjusting furniture and called out numbers to another woman with a clipboard. Waist. Hips. Bust. Inseam. I stood still in my jeans and t-shirt and thought about how my philosophy professor once described Descartes' separation of mind and body, how the mind is the self and the body is the machine. In that room, the machine was all anyone cared about. They did not ask me a single question about what I thought or knew or wanted. They asked me to turn around.

I got signed. Not to anything significant. Catalog work, test shoots, the occasional fitting for a designer whose name I almost recognized. But the signing itself was a door, and behind it was a world that had been running without me, a world I had not known existed in the specific way you don't know a city exists until someone hands you a map.

The parties started almost immediately. Not because of the agency but because of the ecosystem. You go to one casting and meet a girl who invites you to a thing in the Hills. You go to the thing in the Hills and meet a photographer who invites you to a thing in Silver Lake. You go to the thing in Silver Lake and someone takes your picture and it shows up on The Cobra Snake the next morning, your face among thirty other faces, performing the exact same expression of curated nonchalance.

I learned the expression quickly. Chin down. Eyes up. Mouth slightly open, like you've just said something clever and are waiting for the room to catch up. Don't smile too wide. Don't look too eager. Don't look at the camera like you want it. Look at the camera like you're doing it a favor.

Los Angeles was good at this: teaching you to perform indifference while caring desperately. Every room I entered that year had a velvet rope or a list or a door guy who made you wait just long enough to wonder if you were important. And then you were inside, and the music was too loud, and the drinks were free if you were pretty enough or knew the right person, and the whole thing hummed with an energy that felt like significance but was actually just proximity to people who might be significant.

I was, for a brief period, proximate.

The men arrived with the rooms. They were already there when I got there, leaning against bars and DJ booths and the hoods of cars parked at angles on narrow Hollywood streets. They were promoters, producers, photographers, club owners, managers of bands I'd never heard of. They wore leather jackets or no jackets or vintage t-shirts that cost three hundred dollars. They had opinions about whiskey and vinyl and neighborhoods east of La Brea. They looked at me the way they looked at everything: appraisingly, as if calculating whether I was worth the effort of a conversation.

Some of them were. Some of them were smart, funny, interesting. One of them told me about Basquiat while we stood on a rooftop in Echo Park, and I thought, briefly, that this was what my twenties were supposed to be. Art and rooftops and ideas and the city spread out beneath us like a promise. For about forty-five minutes I believed I had arrived somewhere worth arriving.

Then his hand moved to my lower back and kept going, and the conversation about Basquiat turned into a negotiation I hadn't agreed to, and I understood that the rooftop and the art and the whiskey and the ideas had been a runway, not a destination. Every beautiful thing in that world was a runway. You walked down it and at the end, someone was waiting to take something.

I kept going to the parties. This is the part that is hard to explain, because from the outside it looks like a choice, and from the inside it felt like gravity. I didn't have anywhere else to go. My apartment was a studio in Koreatown with a window that looked onto another window, and my hostess job ended at midnight, and my roommate from college had already gone to law school in Boston, and my mother was in Northern California managing her own wreckage, and Mark was still alive then but only in the way a fire is alive when it's down to embers. I did not want to sit in my apartment thinking about Mark. I did not want to think about my mother's voice on the phone, the specific thinness of it, the way she described his drinking now with a flatness that was scarier than the panic had been.

So I put on something tight and went to a party.

The fashion was a language I could control. This I understood. In a world where everything about my body was being read by other people, getting dressed was the one act of authorship I had. I chose every piece deliberately: the vintage Helmut Lang blazer from a consignment shop on Fairfax, the heels that made me two inches taller than most of the men in the room, the earrings my grandmother had given me that I wore like a talisman against the version of femininity being sold everywhere else. I dressed like armor, like argument, like a sentence I was constructing about who I was, even as the rooms I walked into insisted on reading only the body underneath.

There was a photographer. There is always a photographer. This one had a studio in a converted warehouse on the east side, exposed brick, diffused light, a claw-foot bathtub in the corner that he used as a prop. He shot editorial. He shot for magazines whose names sounded European. He was in his forties and wore his reading glasses on top of his head and called everyone "babe." The shoot started normally. A few test frames. Adjustments. "Turn left. Chin up. Good." And then, slowly, the directions changed. "Unbutton that. Just one. Let the fabric fall." And then: "The light's hitting your shoulder wrong. Can you pull the strap down?" Each instruction was reasonable in isolation. Each one, by itself, was a professional request made in a professional tone. But the sequence was a sentence, and the sentence was moving in one direction, and I knew where it ended because I had been reading rooms since I was five years old. My body knew before my mind did. I felt my shoulders tighten, felt the familiar electricity of my nervous system reclassifying a situation from safe to not safe. I said I needed a break. He said, "We're almost there, babe. Just a few more." As if stopping were the unreasonable thing. As if I were the one being difficult.

I finished the shoot. He got his frames. I got a check for four hundred dollars and a sick feeling that lasted for weeks.

The night I remember most clearly is not the worst one. It was ordinary, which is what made it so perfectly terrible.

A house party in Laurel Canyon. Someone's boyfriend's producer's house, one of those possessive chains that passed for credentials. The house was all glass and cantilevered wood, perched on a hillside, the kind of architecture that looks like it's daring gravity to try something. Lady Gaga was playing through speakers embedded in the walls. The pool was lit from below, that chemical turquoise that makes everything look like a movie about Los Angeles. Girls in cutoff shorts and boots stood near the water holding drinks they hadn't paid for. A DJ set up in the corner of the living room, though no one was dancing, because dancing would have meant wanting something too visibly.

I knew maybe three people. I knew them the way I knew everyone that year: well enough to hug, not well enough to call. I got a drink. Vodka something. The bartender was someone's friend doing it as a favor, and he poured heavy, which was either generosity or a strategy. I stood on the deck and looked at the city below, and the city looked back with ten thousand lights, each one a room where someone was performing their life for an audience of people doing the same thing.

A man found me near the bathroom. I was waiting in the narrow hallway, one shoulder against the wall, checking my phone. He positioned himself between me and the rest of the party. Casual. Friendly. He told me I looked familiar. Had I been in something? Was I an actress? A model? I said I modeled sometimes and he said he could tell, he had an eye for it, and then he moved closer and the hallway got smaller and his hand landed on the wall next to my head and he leaned in and said something about how I should smile more because I had a beautiful smile. The classic. The old reliable. The line that sounds like a compliment and functions like a cage, because the moment a man tells you to smile, what he is actually telling you is that your face belongs to him.

I smiled. Of course I smiled. I had been smiling my way out of corners since I was old enough to understand that corners existed. I said something light, something deflecting, something that communicated disinterest without the sharpness that might provoke him, and I slipped under his arm and went back to the party and got another drink and stayed another two hours because leaving felt like losing. I don't know what I would have been losing. The evening. The illusion that I was having fun. The idea that these rooms were where my life was happening. Whatever it was, I couldn't let go of it yet.

Lana Del Rey was playing now. "Video Games." The slow, drowning sadness of it filling the house like smoke while everyone around me pretended they weren't drowning too. A girl I vaguely knew from a shoot was taking a selfie by the pool, angling her phone until the light caught her jaw correctly, and I watched her take the same photo nine times and choose the one where she looked the most like she wasn't trying. This was 2012, maybe 2013. Instagram was still new enough to feel like a choice rather than a requirement. But already I could see what it was becoming. The same appraisal that happened in casting rooms and clubs and the back seats of Town Cars was now something you could carry in your pocket. Beauty quantified. Worth measured in a number beneath a photograph. I had an account. I posted. I watched the likes accumulate with a feeling that was not quite pleasure and not quite emptiness but something in the exact center, a sensation for which no one had yet invented a name.

I thought about Chatroulette. The year before, maybe two years before, I had spent nights cycling through strangers on that site, clicking through faces the way you flip channels. Most of it was men. Most of them were naked or getting there. The randomness was the appeal, the absolute chaos of not knowing what would appear when you hit next. It felt like the internet's version of the parties: you showed up, you performed a self, you were appraised, you moved on. The male gaze replicated at the speed of a mouse click. I told myself I was doing it out of boredom, but the truth was more complicated. I was looking for something, or avoiding something, and the constant motion of new faces kept me from having to figure out which.

That night in Laurel Canyon, I left around two. The cab smelled like pine air freshener and the driver played something quiet on the radio. I sat in the back seat and watched the city scroll past the window, the billboards and taco stands and the long dark stretches of Sunset that don't make it into the movies. I had lipstick on. I had a phone full of photos someone had taken of me. I had a story to tell about the party if anyone asked. From the outside, I was a young woman living a life in Los Angeles, and the life looked like exactly what a life in Los Angeles should look like.

From the inside, I was running.

I was running from the apartment in Koreatown where the silence reminded me of things I could not hold. I was running from my mother's phone calls. I was running from the feeling that surfaced every time the party ended and the music stopped and I was alone with whatever was underneath, which was something large and dark and patient and had been waiting for me since I was a child standing in a kitchen watching adults fall apart. I put on the Helmut Lang blazer and I went to the parties and I let the rooms full of strangers fill the space where the grief was supposed to go, because I did not know yet what the grief was for. I knew something was wrong at home. I knew Mark was disappearing into something. I knew my mother's voice was getting thinner. But I did not know the shape of what was coming, so I did what I had always done. I performed the next hour. I smiled at the right people. I kept moving.

The next morning I woke in my apartment and looked at myself in the bathroom mirror. Mascara smudged under my eyes. Foundation creased into the lines of my pillow. My hair still stiff with whatever product the girl at the pre-party had sprayed into it. I looked like a photograph of a person rather than a person. I looked like one of those Cobra Snake images, frozen in a moment of performed joy, except the moment was over and the joy had been exported and all that remained was the face underneath, which was tired and twenty-three and quietly desperate.

I washed my face. The makeup came off in brown rivulets, circling the drain. Underneath was skin. Just skin. No label, no brand, no angle, no light. The face that no one at the party would have recognized, because the party version of me was a construction, a careful assembly of references and signals and the exact right shade of disinterest, and this, the face in the mirror, was just a girl who had studied Hegel and couldn't sleep and missed her mother and was afraid of something she could not name.

I thought about Black Swan. I had watched it in a theater on La Brea a few months earlier, alone, matinee showing, popcorn I didn't eat. Natalie Portman's character destroying herself in pursuit of perfection. The version of beauty that consumed the person inside it. I had sat in the dark theater and felt something recognition-shaped move through my chest, not because I was a dancer or an artist but because I understood the mathematics of it: the world tells you that your body is the instrument, and you practice and starve and contort until the instrument is flawless, and then the instrument is all that's left, and the person who once lived inside it has been refined out of existence.

Philosophy was the opposite of everything in those rooms. In a seminar, what you thought mattered more than how you looked. An argument was evaluated on its structure, not its packaging. Hegel didn't care about your cheekbones. Kant was indifferent to your waistline. I had spent four years in classrooms where the interior life was the subject, and then I had walked into a world that only saw the exterior, and the dissonance was so loud I could feel it in my teeth.

But I kept going. That is the part I still cannot reconcile. I knew the rooms were hollow. I knew the parties were performances. I knew that the men who looked at me were not seeing me, not the me that read Kierkegaard on my lunch breaks, not the me that called her mother every Sunday and listened to the pauses more carefully than the words. They saw a face, a body, a surface, and they interacted with the surface the way you interact with a vending machine: insert attention, expect product.

I kept going because the alternative was stillness, and stillness meant feeling, and feeling meant the thing about Mark that I could not yet say.

---

Across the lawn, the woman in the gold dress is showing someone her phone. A photo, probably. Or the photo of a photo. The recursive performance of beauty: the image of the image of the image, each layer thinner than the last.

I catch myself adjusting my posture. Shoulders back. Chin lifted. The old alignment, the one that was trained into my body by cameras and casting directors and the hallways of Hollywood houses. The muscle memory is still there, lodged somewhere between my spine and my self-consciousness, and when a beautiful woman enters a room my body still responds before my mind can intervene.

I let my shoulders drop. I let the posture go. It releases like a held breath.

I am not that girl anymore. The girl in the bathroom mirror with the mascara smudged and the emptiness behind her eyes, the girl who mistook proximity for arrival, who thought that if the room was beautiful enough, she might be beautiful enough, and if she was beautiful enough, she might be enough.

I am also still her. I will always be a little bit still her. The difference is that now I know what I was running from, and I know that no room, however gilded, was ever going to outrun it. The parties were not freedom. They were the most expensive way I ever found to hide.

Ethan's mother's friend is still beside me, smiling, waiting for something. I realize I never really answered her.

"I teach high school English now," I say.

She nods politely. The nod says: what a waste.

I let it land. I let it sit there between us, the quiet verdict of a woman who thinks beauty is something you spend and teaching is something you settle for. She is not wrong that I was beautiful. She is not wrong that I could have stayed. She is wrong about everything else.
