# Chapter 9
## The Party

Jess is telling a story with her whole body. Both hands in the air, champagne tilting dangerously, voice pitched to carry across three conversations. Something about a student who turned in a paper written entirely in second person because, he told her, "first person felt too vulnerable." She delivers the punchline leaning backward, nearly colliding with a caterer carrying a tray of bruschetta. Rachel steadies the champagne glass without breaking eye contact. My sister stands slightly apart, arms crossed, smiling the way she smiles at parties: warmly, from a safe distance.

I watch them. My bridesmaids. The word itself feels borrowed, a term from someone else's story that I am still learning to say without flinching. Jess, who drove me to the ER during a panic attack in my second year of teaching and never mentioned it again. Rachel, who sat across from me in a college dining hall while I cried into a bowl of cereal about Will and said, "You need to eat the cereal or throw it away, but you can't just cry into it." My sister, who knows things about me that neither of them will ever know and has chosen, gracefully and permanently, not to discuss any of them.

Three women. Three fragments. Jess has never heard the word "hospitalization" in connection to my name. Rachel knows about Will but not about the diagnosis. My sister knows about both but would rather hold a hot coal than talk about feelings at a wedding reception. Each of them holds a piece of me that the others have never touched. Standing here watching them laugh together, I feel the strange arithmetic of my adult friendships: the sum is real, but the parts never add up to a whole. No one in this room has the complete story. I am not sure I have the complete story. I am not sure the complete story is something one person is meant to hold.

A champagne glass finds its way into my hand. I do not remember picking it up. The bubbles rise in thin lines toward the surface. Somewhere behind me, a speaker plays a song I half recognize, and the sound of it, the low beat mixed with laughter mixed with the clink of glass on glass, reaches into a place I keep locked, and turns the handle. The first drink I ever held was not champagne. It was a warm Bud Light at a party I was too young to be at, and before that drink, before all the drinks, there were the mornings.

I was fifteen. Maybe sixteen. Old enough to know something was wrong with me, too young to know it had a name. Every day started the same way: the alarm went off and I checked it. Then checked it again. Then a third time, because twice felt like a dare, like leaving a door open just wide enough for something terrible to walk through. Three times. Always three. Two was an invitation. One was suicide. Three was the minimum the universe required to let me survive until lunch.

The lock on the front door. Three times. I would stand in the hallway with my backpack on, hand on the deadbolt, turning it to feel the click. Click, click, click. My sister would be waiting in the car already. Some mornings I could hear her honk. But I could not leave until the number was right, until my hand and the lock and the universe had completed their negotiation, and if I lost count or got distracted, I had to start over. Three. Always from the beginning.

At school the ceiling had those white acoustic tiles with a pattern of tiny holes, and during lectures I would count them. I could not stop counting them. The teacher's voice would be somewhere far away, talking about the Missouri Compromise or mitosis, and I would be locked onto a tile directly above my head, running the holes in rows. Twelve across. Eighteen down. Two hundred and sixteen. I would do this for the entire period. If the number came out odd I felt a thin blade of panic press against the inside of my chest and I would recount, and if it came out different the second time, I would sit perfectly still and breathe through my mouth and tell myself the ceiling was not going to collapse, that the number did not matter, that I was insane and no one could see it.

No one could see it. That was the thing. I performed normalcy the way my mother performed composure: flawlessly, without rehearsal, because the alternative was unthinkable. I sat in class and nodded at the right moments and raised my hand and turned in homework that I had rewritten three times because the first two drafts contained letters that looked wrong. Not incorrect. Wrong. The curve of a lowercase *a* that did not close properly. The tail of a *y* that dipped too far below the line. The handwriting had to be right or the sentence could not mean what it was supposed to mean, and I knew this was irrational, I knew it even then, the way you can know the stove is off and still need to check it, and the knowing changes nothing. The knowing is just another room in the same prison.

My notebooks had a worn spot on the left margin where I erased and rewrote the date. Four times, sometimes five. The paper thinned there, nearly transparent, a small window into the desk beneath. If anyone noticed they said nothing. The quiet girl with the neat handwriting and the good grades and the faint gray smudge on every page.

At night the real work began.

I would lie in bed with the ceiling dark above me and my jaw already clenching and I would begin the audit. My mother driving to work: what if a truck ran a red light. My sister walking to a friend's house: what if someone followed her. Mark in the garage, where the sound of a bottle being set down carried through the floor. I would lie there and count the seconds between sounds, listening for proof he was still upright. The house: what if there was a gas leak. What if there was a fire. What if I left the stove on, even though I had not used the stove, even though I had checked the knobs before bed, three times, always three. My brain ran through catastrophes the way other people scrolled through television channels, clicking from one to the next, lingering on the worst ones. The scenarios were vivid. I could see the ambulance lights. I could see my mother's face on the stretcher. I could see the flames through the bedroom window. The images arrived unbidden, uninvited, projected onto the inside of my skull with a clarity that felt like prophecy.

The clock at 3 a.m. glowing red on the nightstand. The particular texture of exhaustion that comes from spending four hours inside a mind that will not stop running its simulations. My jaw ached from clenching. I pressed my tongue to the roof of my mouth and tried to unclench, and the muscles would release for a moment and then seize again, a fist I could not open.

Sometimes an intrusive thought would break through the loop. A flash of something violent. Someone I loved getting hurt. My own hands doing something terrible. The images were not mine. They arrived like signals from a station I had not tuned to, vivid and grotesque, and the shame of having them was worse than the images themselves. What kind of person thinks these things? What kind of brain produces, unprompted, a scene of harm and replays it in high definition until you want to claw the thought out of your own head?

I thought I was broken in a way that was specifically mine: a defect in the machinery, a wire crossed somewhere deep in the architecture of who I was. I thought the counting and the checking and the catastrophizing were the price I paid for being alive, and I thought everyone else was paying a similar price and simply not talking about it, or I thought I was the only one, and either possibility was equally lonely.

I was so tired. Not the kind of tired that sleep fixes. The kind that lives in the bones, in the muscles behind the eyes. I went to school. I came home. I checked the lock. I counted the tiles. I lay in the dark. I got up. I did it again.

And then someone handed me a drink.

---

Getting ready for a party was its own kind of ritual.

The outfit first. I stood in front of my closet the way I stood in front of the lock: with absolute focus. A pair of low-rise jeans from Hollister that I had saved for by babysitting the Garcias' kids three weekends in a row. A top I cannot name now but can still feel: thin straps, a blue so dark it was almost black, something that showed my collarbone. Converse, the black ones, the laces fraying. I dressed like I was constructing a person, which I was. The girl in the mirror was not the girl who counted ceiling tiles. She was someone else. Someone who went to parties and laughed easily and did not need to touch the doorframe before leaving the room.

The eyeliner took twenty minutes. Not because I was bad at it. Because it had to be right, and right meant symmetrical, and symmetrical meant doing it over and over until both eyes matched in a way that satisfied something deeper than aesthetics. My sister's voice from down the hall: "We're going to be late." She was always ready first. She applied makeup the way she did everything: efficiently, without existential crisis.

I looked in the mirror. The girl looking back was acceptable. Not beautiful, not yet, not in the way I would later learn to perform, but passable. Present. Someone who might belong at a party if she didn't talk too much or stand in the wrong corner or let her face do the thing it did when her brain started its audit.

The party was at somebody's house. There was always a somebody, and the somebody always had parents who were out of town or who didn't care or who existed in that permissive fog where teenage drinking was handled with the logic of inevitability: they're going to do it anyway. A dark living room. Music I felt before I heard it, the bass coming up through the floorboards into the soles of my shoes. Bodies moving in the half-light. The smell of cheap beer and someone's older brother's cologne and something sweet, maybe a candle, maybe just the sugar of whatever someone had mixed with vodka.

The first drink was warm Bud Light from a can that someone pressed into my hand near the kitchen doorway. I did not like the taste. This is the detail I always come back to, the part that matters: I did not like the taste. It was bitter and flat and vaguely metallic, and I drank it anyway, because the taste was not the point.

The point was what happened next.

The counting stopped.

Not gradually. Not the way a headache fades or a muscle unclenches. It stopped the way a television screen goes black when you pull the plug. One moment, the ticker running across the bottom of my brain, the endless scroll of numbers and checks and what-ifs. The next moment: silence. An actual, physical silence inside my own skull, as if someone had opened a window in a room that had been sealed for years and the air rushed in and everything went still.

I stood in that kitchen holding a can of warm beer, and for the first time in my conscious memory, my brain was quiet.

I have tried to explain this to people who do not have OCD, and the explanation always falls short. The best I can do is this: imagine you have been listening to an alarm clock ringing since you were five years old. Not a loud alarm. Just a steady, insistent beep, always there, never stopping, running underneath every conversation and every class and every meal and every attempt at sleep. You have never known what the world sounds like without it. You have built your entire life around the sound. And then one night, in a stranger's kitchen, someone hands you a drink, and the alarm stops.

You would drink too.

The second beer tasted better than the first. By the third, the world had softened at its edges, the way a photograph blurs when you pull the lens out of focus, and the blur was beautiful. The music was no longer sound but texture, something I could lean into. The people around me became less threatening, their faces less detailed, their judgments less precise. The girl with the eyeliner, the Converse, the careful outfit, she loosened. She laughed at a joke she would normally have analyzed for hidden meaning. She leaned against a wall and talked to a boy whose name she would not remember tomorrow and did not care.

The anxiety did not disappear. It receded. Like a tide pulling back from the shore, exposing sand that had been underwater for years. I could see the ground. I could stand on it. I could breathe.

The first drink silenced the alarm. The second softened the world. The third gave me a personality I could tolerate. For a few hours on a Friday night, in somebody's living room with the lights off and the music loud and the windows steaming with the heat of bodies, I was a normal teenager. Not performing normalcy. Not running the audit while smiling. Actually normal. Actually free.

The pattern formed quickly, the way patterns do when they meet a need that has been unmet for years. During the week: school, the lock, the tiles, the letters, the 3 a.m. catastrophes, the jaw, the exhaustion. On the weekend: relief. Friday night became the hinge on which my entire week swung. Every ceiling tile I counted was endured because Friday was coming. Every rewritten date, every checked lock, every intrusive image of violence projected onto the inside of my eyelids at midnight. Endurable. Because Friday.

I noticed, without letting myself fully notice, that the anxiety came back worse on Saturdays. The mornings after were louder. The counting more insistent. The lock needed four checks instead of three, as if the compulsions were punishing me for having escaped them. The hangover was not just physical. It was mechanical: the machine restarting, recalibrating, running harder to make up for the hours it had been shut down. Which meant by Saturday afternoon I was already thinking about the next Friday. The next party. The next drink. The cycle feeding itself.

There was a moment, at a party in someone's backyard, when I saw my mother's hand. Not literally. I was holding a red plastic cup and I looked down at my own fingers wrapped around it and I saw her fingers around the stem of a wineglass on a Sunday evening, and the recognition hit me so fast I could not look away. The inherited gesture. The same grip. The same loose hold on something that was doing exactly the same work: making the noise stop, softening the edges, turning the volume down on a life that was too loud to bear without help.

I put the cup down. Then I picked it up again. Because what else was I going to do. The alternative was the lock. The tiles. The 3 a.m. clock. The jaw aching in the dark. I was sixteen years old and no one had given me a better option.

The friendships I made at parties were real in the way fireworks are real: bright, vivid, gone by morning. Girls I hugged in bathrooms and told I loved. Boys I kissed against fences and forgot. Conversations that felt profound at midnight and evaporated with the hangover. We did not know each other. We knew the versions of each other that only existed between eleven p.m. and two a.m., in dark rooms, with the music doing most of the emotional work. By Monday those people passed me in the hallway and we nodded, and the nod was an agreement: what happened at the party was a separate life. A parallel self. We did not owe that self anything in daylight.

---

The champagne is cool in my hand. Small bubbles breaking at the surface.

Across the room, Will is laughing at something Rachel has said. Not his polite laugh, the measured one he uses at work events, but the real one, sudden and full, the one that sounds like it surprises even him. His head tilts back and his hand comes up to rub the back of his neck, and for a moment he is just a man laughing at a party, unguarded, and the sight of it catches somewhere beneath my ribs.

I take a sip. It tastes like what it is: expensive, dry, celebratory. Not like warm Bud Light in a stranger's kitchen. Not like the red plastic cups of someone's backyard. The bubbles do not reach the place that the beer once reached. The alarm, these days, is managed by other means: medication, therapy, the daily practice of living inside a brain I have learned to negotiate with rather than silence.

But my body remembers. The first swallow, the familiar warmth spreading down through my chest, the ghost of relief that is no longer relief but muscle memory. My body remembers what my mind has spent years relearning. That the quiet was borrowed. That the cost was always compounding.

Jess throws her arm around my shoulder, hard enough that the champagne in her glass sloshes. "You're doing the thing," she says. "The standing-in-the-corner-looking-like-a-Victorian-ghost thing. Stop it. You're getting married, not writing a dissertation." She squeezes my shoulder the way she shows love: with force, with volume, with the same reckless generosity that made her drive ninety on the freeway to get me to the ER during a panic attack and then sit in the waiting room for three hours playing Candy Crush without once asking what was wrong. She never asks what is wrong. She just shows up with her whole body and dares the crisis to continue in her presence.

Rachel is quieter. She stands on my other side, adjusting the strap of my dress with the precision of someone who has been fixing small things her entire life. She does not throw her arm around me. She corrects. She steadies. She watches me the way she has watched me since college, with the careful attention of a woman who saw the full arc of Will the first time, who held me in the dining hall while I cried into cereal, who knows things about my history that Jess never will and carries that knowledge the way she carries everything: carefully, without drama, without ever quite saying what she thinks. She sees the champagne in my hand and her eyes flicker to it and back to my face, and the flicker is so fast that only someone who has been observed this closely for fifteen years would catch it. She is not judging. She is tracking. She has always been tracking.

My sister catches my eye from across the circle, and her look says what her mouth will not: *I see you. I know where you just went.*

I set the glass down. Half full. The bubbles keep rising.
